Clarifying statements about leadership

  • Leadership != Management
  • Influence as a leader != Manipulation through authority
  • Leadership is about integrity, vision, and getting others to voluntarily follow you
  • A lot of leaders do not have formal management positions or authority in the same sense that managers do.
  • Managers who are not good leaders are very unlikely to be successful in the long-term.
  • While a manager may have formal authority to force or otherwise manipulate others to take some specific action, that is not at all what I'm referring to as I talk about influence throughout this presentation.
  • Instead, influence as a leader is about the ability to motivate others to voluntarily follow you.

Fragmented scheduling challenges

  • Finding time for Deep work
    • Requires uninterrupted focus (or else)
    • Typically the most important tasks we handle; can't typically delegate
    • Cannot be easily done in short bursts
    • Read Deep Work by Cal Newport to learn more
  • Inefficiencies due to context switching
  • Mental focus / Mind conditioned to be easily distracted
  • Some of the specific challenges I've faced with fragmented schedules...
  • Deep work? What is that? In comparison to shallow work...
  • Fragmented schedules cause a lot of context switching. Loss of productivity.
  • Easy for our minds to be used to small periods of shallow work, making it difficult to focus deeply for long periods of time (efficiently). Find myself feeling the need to complete something small to feel like I'm doing something.

Maintain calendar control

  • Avoid unnecessary meetings
  • Be smart/cautious regarding recurring series
  • Batch meetings to limit fragmentation
  • Reserve blocks of time to focus
  • Give yourself time to recharge; protect your lunch
  • Things I've done that people I've worked with will have likely noticed
  • Done these things without getting pushback from others
  • Be wary of attending anything that can proceed as usual without you present

Prioritize your work

  1. Urgent and important
  2. Not urgent but important
  3. Urgent but not important
  4. Not urgent and not important

Read Habit 3: Begin with the end in mind in The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People to learn more.

  • Urgent and important - the fires that you truly do HAVE to fight
  • Not urgent but important - a lot of your planning/preparation and proactive efforts are in this category; ignore it and watch it suddenly become urgent
  • Urgent but not important - do as little of this as possible; typically for someone else's goals
  • Not urgent and not important - avoid at all costs
  • Strive to maximize your time spent on the not urgent but important work

Learning to say no to...

  • Common assumptions about your work:
    • You HAVE to do it all
    • It's all important
    • You CAN do it all
  • Requests for assistance (email, IM, in-person) that are not important or urgent
  • Opportunities that you're not passionate about; leave space for what truly matters
  • Things that you don't HAVE to be present for

Read Essentialism to learn more

Working deliberately vs reactively

  • Avoid operating in auto-pilot
  • Choose what to work on and when
    • Consider importance relative to goals
    • Consider required energy/focus levels
  • Do not shape your day around your inbox
    • Check email at predefined intervals and stay out of it otherwise
    • (I'm terrible at this and fight the urge constantly)

Achieving balance in responsibilities

  • Review regular tasks and consider relevancy to role
  • Limit tasks unnecessary/unrelated to higher level goals
  • Focus majority of time in most important areas
  • If volume of tasks or frequency of interruptions causes difficulty, block calendar or escape to create space
  • As I wrote and reviewed my latest tasks while working on the presentation, I stopped about 20 items in and noted that email was nowhere on my list. Based on my default mode of operation (when I'm not focusing explicitly on doing otherwise), you would think it was at the top of my list of responsibilities.

Increased need for effective communication

  • The content thus far has been not so much about leadership, but more about becoming effective in your role as you take on additional leadership responsibilities.
  • This is intentional, as it is difficult to be an effective leader if you are not at least personally effective. A lot of literature on communication, leadership, and personal growth puts an intentional focus on refining internal (intrapersonal) skills as the initial foundation upon which external (interpersonal) skills are developed. I've utilized the same approach here, so now we will move on to some interpersonal concepts, particularly communication here and then collaboration in just a bit.
"Communication is where leadership lives and breathes."
- Gary Burnison
  • Communication is how you connect with, motivate, and influence others.
  • Effective leadership requires clear, credible, and authentic communication.
  • Can you even recall someone who you'd willingly follow who was terrible at communication?
  • Considering these ideas, let's talk further on how we can ensure that our communication efforts aid, rather than hamper, our role as leaders.
